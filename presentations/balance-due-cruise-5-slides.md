# Balance Due in the Cruise Industry

## Slide 1: Introduction — What Is Balance Due?
- The remaining payment owed after an initial deposit has been made on a cruise booking
- Part of the standard cruise booking lifecycle: deposit → balance due → sailing
- Critical touchpoint for cruise lines, travel agents, and passengers alike

*Image placeholder: [Image of a cruise ship at sea](assets/images/cruise-ship.jpg)*  
*Speaker Notes: Set the scene by explaining the typical cruise booking journey. Emphasise that understanding balance due is essential for a smooth customer experience and revenue management.*

## Slide 2: How Balance Due Is Calculated
- **Formula:** Total fare (cruise fare + taxes/fees + add-ons) − Deposit paid = Balance due
- Add-ons that increase the balance: shore excursions, beverage/dining packages, travel insurance, gratuities
- Currency considerations for international bookings — exchange rates can affect the final amount

*Image placeholder: [Image of a booking invoice or calculator](assets/images/booking-invoice.jpg)*  
*Speaker Notes: Walk through a worked example — e.g., $3,500 total fare minus $500 deposit = $2,000 balance due. Mention that guests should review their booking confirmation carefully for itemised costs.*

---

## Slide 3: Balance Due Dates — Industry Standards
- Most cruise lines require full payment **60–120 days** before departure
- Luxury and expedition lines typically require payment **90–120 days** out
- Last-minute bookings (inside the final-payment window): full fare is due immediately
- Group bookings may have customised schedules negotiated with the cruise line

*Image placeholder: [Image of a calendar highlighting a payment deadline](assets/images/payment-calendar.jpg)*  
*Speaker Notes: Highlight that the due date varies by cruise line, itinerary length, and booking type. Agents should always check the specific policy for each reservation and communicate it clearly to guests.*

---

## Slide 4: Consequences of Missing the Balance Due Date
- **Automatic cancellation:** reservation is released back into inventory if payment is not received
- **Deposit forfeiture:** standard cancellation policies typically retain the deposit after the deadline
- **Rebooking risks:** original cabin category, pricing, and promotions may no longer be available
- **Agent impact:** potential commission charge-backs and damage to client relationships

*Image placeholder: [Image depicting a cancelled booking notification](assets/images/cancelled-booking.jpg)*  
*Speaker Notes: Stress the importance of proactive communication. A missed balance due date is one of the most common — and avoidable — causes of booking loss in the cruise industry.*

---

## Slide 5: Best Practices for Managing Balance Due
- Set up **automated reminders** at 90, 60, and 30 days before the due date
- Offer **multiple payment options:** credit/debit card, bank transfer, or instalment plans where available
- Recommend **travel insurance** at the time of deposit to protect against cancellation before the balance is paid
- Confirm payment receipt promptly and issue a **revised booking confirmation** to the guest

*Image placeholder: [Image of a travel agent reviewing a booking checklist with a client](assets/images/agent-checklist.jpg)*  
*Speaker Notes: Close with actionable takeaways. Proactive reminders, clear communication, and flexible payment options significantly reduce the risk of cancellations and improve guest satisfaction.*
