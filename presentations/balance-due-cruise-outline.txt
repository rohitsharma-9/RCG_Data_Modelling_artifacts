Balance Due in the Cruise Industry (What it is & why it matters)
	Balance due is the remaining payment owed on a cruise booking after the initial deposit has been applied.
	Booking lifecycle: inquiry → deposit → balance due → sailing date.
	Why it matters: timely payment protects the booking, avoids automatic cancellation, and ensures onboard credits or promotions remain valid.

How Balance Due Is Calculated
	Total fare (cruise fare + taxes + fees + optional add-ons) minus deposit paid = balance due.
	Add-ons that affect balance: shore excursions pre-booked, drink/dining packages, travel insurance, port charges.
	Currency and exchange-rate considerations for international passengers.

Balance Due Dates & Industry Standards
	Most major cruise lines require full payment 60–120 days before departure (varies by line and itinerary length).
	Luxury and expedition cruises often require earlier payment (90–120 days out).
	Last-minute bookings (inside the final-payment window): full fare due at time of booking.
	Group bookings may carry customised payment schedules negotiated with the cruise line.

Consequences of Missing the Balance Due Date
	Automatic cancellation: reservation is released back into inventory if payment is not received by the due date.
	Loss of deposit: standard cancellation policies typically forfeit the deposit after the final-payment deadline passes.
	Rebooking risks: cabin category or pricing may no longer be available; promotional rates usually cannot be re-applied.
	Impact on travel agents: commission charge-backs and client relationship damage.

Best Practices for Managing Balance Due
	Automated reminders: send notifications at 90, 60, and 30 days before the due date.
	Multiple payment options: credit/debit card, bank transfer, instalment plans where offered.
	Travel insurance: recommend at time of deposit to cover cancellation risk before balance is paid.
	Agent responsibilities: confirm payment receipt, update booking records, and issue revised confirmation to guests.
